5 human filters for a job offer text

The core of the service is simple: slow the message down and check it like a careful person would. Not every odd text is a scam, but every risky request deserves a pause.

1. Identity

Who is writing

Company name, official website, company email, recruiter identity, and a job post you can verify outside the chat.

2. Offer

What is promised

Role, tasks, schedule, pay, contract, interview process, and who will actually sign or approve the work.

3. Request

What they want first

Fees, deposits, bank details, ID documents, login codes, device access, apps, links, or fake check steps.

4. Pressure

How they push timing

Urgent hiring, limited spots, secrecy, pressure to reply now, or anger when you ask normal verification questions.

5. Reply

What to ask next

A calm message that asks for verifiable details before you send documents, pay fees, open files, or click links.

Outcome

A slower decision

You leave with risk signals, verification questions, and a safer next step instead of guessing under pressure.

Job offer scam red flags

One signal is not proof. Several of these together are a good reason to stop and verify before replying or sending anything sensitive.

  • Money before work. Training fees, equipment deposits, account activation, a "refundable" payment, or a fee to receive wages.
  • Missing company trail. Personal Gmail, no company domain, no public job post, vague legal entity, or a website that does not match the message.
  • Easy income for vague tasks. High pay for simple likes, reviews, crypto tasks, reshipping, data entry, or "assistant" work with no interview.
  • Sensitive data too early. ID photos, bank details, card images, tax numbers, login codes, or a request to install remote-access tools.
  • Urgency and secrecy. "Reply now", "limited slots", "do not tell anyone", or pressure to move to WhatsApp or Telegram immediately.

Do not send yet

A real employer can explain the process and provide verifiable details. Until then, keep the risky parts out of the conversation.

  • Payments or deposits. Do not pay to get hired, receive equipment, unlock tasks, or release wages.
  • ID and payment details. Do not send ID photos, card images, bank logins, crypto wallet recovery phrases, or one-time codes.
  • Unknown files or apps. Do not open archives, APKs, EXEs, macros, or "task documents" from an unverified sender.
  • Fake check steps. Be careful if they send a check and ask you to buy equipment, gift cards, crypto, or return part of the money.

FAQ

Can this prove a recruiter is a scammer?

No. The review highlights risk signals and verification questions. It helps you avoid dangerous next steps without making a legal accusation.

Are WhatsApp or Telegram recruiter texts always fake?

No. But moving straight to a private messenger can hide the company trail, so it is worth asking for official company details before acting.

Can I send screenshots?

Yes, but hide personal data first: names, phone numbers, addresses, payment details, IDs, and login information.
