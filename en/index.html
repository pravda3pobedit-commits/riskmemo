When to check a message

This is for the moment when something feels wrong, but you do not want to overreact or miss a real opportunity.

Jobs

Fake job offer or employer

Telegram job posts, remote work offers, or requests to pay for training, cards, access, or fees.

Freelance

Unclear client or project

A client pushes urgency, asks you to open a file, follow a link, or start work without verifiable details.

Messages

Text that feels templated

The message looks personal, but has little detail, generic wording, and pressure to act quickly.

Money

Investments, crypto, or fast income

Someone promises easy returns, low risk, a bonus after deposit, or a time-limited payment step.

Documents

Requests for ID, cards, or access

You need to know what can be shared, what should be blurred, and what should wait until verification.

Relationships

Fast bonding and money requests

Rapid emotional closeness, pity pressure, urgent help, secrecy, or requests to transfer money.

Difference

Not a fake precision score

I do not give a polished automatic percentage. I review the text and context: what is promised, what is requested, where details are missing, and what can be verified.

Pattern

When a message may be scripted

Templated wording does not prove anything by itself. But when it comes with urgency, money, documents, or access requests, it is a reason to slow down.

How it works

The review is short and practical. The goal is not to accuse someone, but to help you avoid an expensive mistake and ask the right questions.

1

You send the text

A message, job offer, or 5-30 lines of chat. Remove or replace personal data first.

2

I mark risk signals

Urgency, contradictions, missing verifiable data, scripted wording, pressure, and requests for money, documents, or access.

3

You get a practical reply

A compact review: what is concerning, what to verify, and a calm message you can send back.

What you get

The output is not "this person is a scammer." It is a focused risk review that helps you make a calmer decision and avoid sharing too much too soon.

Risk signals

Pressure, urgency, contradictions, vague details, templated wording, and requests for money or access.

Verification questions

Questions that make the other side provide concrete details: website, company email, contract, legal entity, and terms.

A calm reply

A ready-to-send message that slows pressure, avoids accusations, and asks for verifiable details.

Example review

Short review of a suspicious job offer

Risk level
Medium / high
Main reason
They ask for a fee before a contract, use no company email, and push urgency.
Warning signs
Scripted text, vague details, pressure to decide quickly, and a request to open an external link.
Verify
Company website, legal entity, company email, where the job is posted, and who signs the contract.
Calm reply
"Happy to discuss. Before sending documents or paying any fees, I need to verify the company and terms. Please send the official website, company email, and the contract or task description."

Important limits

This is not legal advice, a psychological diagnosis, or a guarantee that something is a scam.

The review highlights textual risk signals and helps you form verification questions. The decision remains yours.
